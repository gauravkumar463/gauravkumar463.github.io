From Simulation to Wind Tunnel: Decoding Hypersonic Oscillations at IISC

Hypersonic Wind Tunnel Testing Aero-acoustics Spectral POD Theoretical Modeling Schlieren Imaging

In the world of high-speed aerodynamics, there is a profound difference between seeing a shock wave on a computer screen and capturing its violent dance in a Mach 6 wind tunnel. My journey as a researcher has spanned both. While my doctoral work focused on building the mathematical engines (solvers) to simulate hypersonic flow, my postdoctoral tenure at the Indian Institute of Science (IISC) took me into the laboratory to validate, analyze, and model the complex physics of aero-acoustics and flow oscillations.

The Research Link: Completing the Loop

My PhD research established the fundamental "modes of unsteadiness" (Pulsation, Oscillation, Vibration) using numerical methods. At IISC, I bridged this with experimental reality. By moving from the Double Wedge (PhD) to the Double Cone (Postdoc), I extended my previous findings to axisymmetric geometries, proving that the physical mechanisms of shock-induced separation are universal, whether simulated in a digital environment or measured in a physical tunnel.

The Postdoc Challenge: Experimental Mastery at Mach 6

Working at the Turbulent Shear Flow Physics and Engineering Laboratory, I led investigations into the self-sustained oscillations that occur over double-cone geometries. This required a sophisticated blend of experimental hardware and advanced data science.

Experimental Precision: I utilized high-speed Schlieren imaging to visualize shock-wave interactions at Mach 6. Capturing these transient phenomena requires not just high-end cameras, but an intuitive understanding of flow synchronization and tunnel noise characteristics.
Data Analysis: To extract signal from noise, I applied Spectral Proper Orthogonal Decomposition (SPOD). This allowed me to isolate the dominant energy-containing frequencies of shock motion, moving beyond simple visualization to rigorous statistical analysis.

Aero-acoustics and The Search for "Frequency Scaling"

One of the most significant contributions of my postdoctoral work was the development of a predictive model for frequency scaling. Aero-acoustics in high-speed flows is often driven by a feedback loop between the separation shock and the reattachment point.

By analyzing the θ1-Λ parameter space, I helped develop a mechanistic model that explains how these oscillations scale with geometry. This research is critical for understanding acoustic radiation—the sound generated by shear layer instabilities—which can cause fatigue in hypersonic vehicle components.

Key Postdoctoral Achievement: We identified a "Strouhal number universality" in high-speed flows. My analysis showed that despite the complexity of the geometry, the fundamental frequency of these oscillations follows predictable patterns when scaled against the separation length and local velocity.

Bridging Theory and Practice

My dual background in algorithm development (PhD) and experimental aero-acoustics (Postdoc) allows me to look at aerospace problems from a unique vantage point. I don't just see data; I see the underlying C++ logic and the physical fluid structures interacting simultaneously. This holistic approach is essential for solving the "Separated Flow Problem" that continues to challenge hypersonic flight.
